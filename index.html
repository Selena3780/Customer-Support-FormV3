Important

Trigger:
Customer mentions stress, mental health, feeling overwhelmed, anxiety, depression, emotional distress, or uses concerning emotional language.

Step 1 – Acknowledge and show empathy

Suggested wording:

“I’m really sorry you’re dealing with this. I appreciate you sharing it with me.”

“You’re not alone in feeling this way, and I’m glad you reached out today.”

Guidance: Use a calm tone. Do not minimise feelings or rush the conversation.

Step 2 – Offer support and ask permission

Suggested wording:

“We speak to a lot of customers who are dealing with things like this, and we do have ways we can support you. Would it be okay if I explain what help might be available today?”

If the customer says no, respect this and focus only on the practical support they request.

Step 3 – Gentle safety check (mandatory)

Suggested wording:

“I just want to check - are you safe at the moment?”

  • Ask clearly and once
  • Pause and listen carefully
  • Do not soften or skip this question

When we ask “Are you safe?”, we are checking whether the customer:

  • Is in immediate danger right now, or
  • Is having thoughts about harming themselves today

You are not asking:

  • For details
  • For plans, methods, or history
  • For a medical explanation

A simple yes / no / not sure is enough.

If the customer says YES, they are safe

Explore impact (do not probe):

“Thank you for letting me know. Would you like to tell me a bit about how this is affecting things for you or if theres anything you feel we could do to help?”

Offer practical support:

  • “We can slow things down and take this step by step.”
  • “If it helps, I can summarise rather than asking lots of questions.”
  • “We may be able to offer specialist support if things feel overwhelming.”

Focus on adjustments, referrals, or follow‑ups. Do not ask about diagnoses or medication.

If the customer is unsure or vague

Suggested wording:

“Thank you for being honest. When things feel unclear like that, it’s still important to make sure you have support. Is there someone who comes to mind that you could reach out to for support?”

Treat as increased vulnerability. Offer additional check‑ins or specialist referral.

🚨 If the customer says NO, or mentions suicidal thoughts

Immediate response:

“I’m really glad you told me. I’m very sorry you’re feeling this way.”

Set boundaries clearly and safely:

“I’m not able to give medical help, but I do want to help you get the right support.”

Encourage immediate external support (UK):

  • “You can contact the Samaritans on 116 123 — they’re available 24/7.”
  • “You can also contact NHS 111 for urgent mental health support.”
  • “If you feel at immediate risk, please call 999 or go to A&E right now.”

Offer to stay present (without replacing support):

“If it helps, I can stay here with you while you contact them or think about your next step.”

Agent must:

  • Alert a supervisor immediately
  • Not continue with transactional queries
  • Not promise confidentiality or outcomes

Safe closing (all paths)

Suggested wording:

“Thank you for speaking with me today. You’ve done the right thing by reaching out, and support is available. We also have a flyer with details of organisations you can reach out to if you ever need extra support. Would it be okay if I emailed that to you?”

Important

Trigger:
Customer appears confused, repeats information, struggles to retain details, has difficulty following explanations, contradicts themselves, or says they are forgetful or overwhelmed.

Step 1 – Acknowledge and slow the conversation

Phrase Variations:

  • That’s absolutely fine, we can slow things down.
  • There’s no rush at all, we can take this one step at a time.
  • Thank you for letting me know — I’ll keep things simple.
  • It sounds like this feels a bit overwhelming right now.
  • We can go over things again if that helps.
  • We don’t need to cover everything today.
  • Please take your time, I’m happy to wait.
  • If anything I say isn’t clear, let me know and I’ll explain it differently.
  • I’ll recap things as we go so nothing is missed.

Slow pacing immediately. Use short sentences and one question at a time.

Step 2 – Check understanding (non‑patronising)

Use supportive check‑ins:

  • Would it help if I repeat that in a simpler way?
  • Can I quickly check I explained that clearly?
  • Shall I summarise where we’re up to so far?
  • Does that make sense, or would you like me to go over it again?

Avoid “Do you understand?” or repeating the same wording louder or faster.

Step 3 – Make reasonable adjustments
  • Break information into small chunks
  • Repeat key points using different wording
  • Summarise frequently (“So just to recap…”)
  • Avoid technical or policy language
  • Reduce choices where possible
  • Avoid placing the customer on hold
Step 4 – Consider decision‑making readiness

Agents should not assess mental capacity, but should consider whether the customer can make decisions during this call.

Consider:

  • Are they able to explain decisions back in simple terms?
  • Are they becoming more confused as the call goes on?
  • Would this decision have long‑term consequences?

If not confident, pause progression and avoid permanent or complex arrangements.

Step 5 – Offer additional support (do not assume)

Suggested wording:

  • Some customers find it helpful to have someone they trust involved, we could authorise a third party on your account to assit you, would that help you?
  • We can pause this today and come back once you’ve had time to think.
  • If it helps, I can arrange for information to be confirmed in an email so you can refer back to it.

Ensure clear consent before involving any third party.

Safeguarding escalation (if required)

Escalate if the customer:

  • Cannot retain information at all
  • Appears disorientated or distressed
  • Is unable to understand consequences
  • Is at risk of financial harm if the call continues

Pause action, reassure the customer, and follow internal escalation guidance.

Supportive closing

Suggested wording:

“Before we finish, I’ll just recap what we’ve agreed so nothing is missed. I’ve noted this on your account so you won’t need to repeat yourself. Please get back in touch anytime if you’d like to go over this again.”

  • Summarise regularly (“So just to recap…”)
  • Avoid long reference numbers unless essential
Step 3 – Explain visual limitations clearly

If parts of the process rely on email, text, or online content, explain this transparently and early.

Suggested wording:

“I want to be upfront, we’re a paperless service and a lot of our communication is sent by email or text. Many customers use screen readers or read‑out features on their devices to access these, but I’ll also explain everything verbally today.”

Route A – Email or text using accessibility features

Many customers who are blind or partially sighted use built‑in accessibility tools on their device.

  • Emails and texts can be read aloud using screen readers
  • Messages can be re‑read at the customer’s own pace

Suggested wording:

“If you use accessibility features like screen readers on your phone or computer, our emails and texts will work with those. I’ll make sure they’re simple and match what we discuss.”

Route B – Third‑party authorisation

If the customer would prefer someone else to manage communication or contact us on their behalf:

Suggested wording:

“If it would help, you can authorise someone you trust to speak to us for you. We’d just need your permission recorded first.”

Do not speak to or accept instructions from an unauthorised person.

Route C – Phone support with verbal confirmation

Where possible, complete support verbally during the call.

  • Explain options and outcomes verbally
  • Confirm choices out loud
  • Provide a full verbal recap at the end

Emails or texts should support the call, not replace the explanation, unless agreed otherwise.

What agents must not do
  • Rely solely on visual instructions
  • Say “it’s all in the email” without explanation
  • Assume a customer cannot use email or text
  • Speak to someone else without authorisation
  • Rush through information

FAQ
What counts as a complaint?

Any dissatisfaction linked to financial loss, distress, or inconvenience should be logged as a complaint — select Complaint as the Contact Type to start the guided flow.

How quickly must a complaint be resolved?

Where possible, resolve within 3 business days (First Contact Resolution). If it can't be resolved at first contact, escalate to the specialist Complaints Team and confirm next steps clearly with the customer.

What must I check first on every complaint?

Vulnerability — always check this first, before anything else, in line with Consumer Duty. Use the Vulnerability Support tool to record it.

How do I apply a vulnerability marker?

Open Vulnerability Support from the Tools menu, choose the situation that best fits, tick the relevant actions, then click Generate Vulnerability Note.

Where do I send vulnerability documentation?

Documentation such as medical confirmation should be sent to financialsupport@bumper.co.

How do I set up a temporary or long-term arrangement?

Select Arrangement as the Contact Type, complete the Financial Assessment, then use the Arrangement Assistant tool to calculate affordability and generate the arrangement summary.

How do I calculate a settlement figure?

Select Settlement as the Contact Type, then open the Settlement Assistant from the Tools menu to work out the discount and outstanding balance.

What do I do if I suspect fraud?

Select Fraud as the Contact Type and work through the fraud questions in order — do not make account changes until this is complete.

The customer wants to raise a debt solution (DMP/IVA/etc.)?

Select Debt Solution as the Contact Type and capture the company name and reference number provided.

Where can I find call scripts?

Open Scripts from the Tools menu — these are grouped by skill type (Inbound/Outbound) and scenario.
